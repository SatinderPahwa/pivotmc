When we step into a complex organisation — whether in manufacturing, healthcare, or finance — we often find ourselves navigating a sea of competing truths. Everyone seems certain they see the problem clearly. Yet somehow, solving it feels harder than it should.

Why?

Often, it's not the facts that differ. It's the interpretation.

### Enter the Rashomon effect

Named after Akira Kurosawa's iconic 1950 film *Rashomon*, the term refers to how multiple people can witness the same event and come away with completely different accounts. Each person believes they're telling the truth, but their truths conflict.

In organisations, the Rashomon effect shows up when departments, teams, or individuals interpret the same issue in vastly different ways — based on background, incentives, or domain expertise. It shapes how we identify problems, assign blame, and pursue solutions.

This is precisely where structured methodologies earn their value:

- **TRIZ** separates the *technical contradiction* from the *human narrative* — what physical parameters are actually in conflict?
- **Six Sigma** demands *data* over *opinion* — measurement replaces testimony
- **Systems thinking** maps the *interactions* between perspectives rather than choosing a winner

The Rashomon Effect isn't a failure of communication. It's a signal that the problem is more complex than any single perspective can capture. The right response is not to pick a side — it's to use a method that transcends sides.
