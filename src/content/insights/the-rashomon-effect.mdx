In Akira Kurosawa's 1950 film *Rashomon*, four witnesses describe the same event — and deliver four contradictory accounts. Each is truthful from their own perspective. None is complete.

Quality investigations face the same problem daily.

## When Everyone Is Right and Nothing Gets Fixed

A production defect occurs. Manufacturing says the design spec was ambiguous. Engineering says the process wasn't controlled. Quality says the inspection criteria were clear. The customer says none of that matters — the product failed.

Each perspective contains truth. But if the investigation stays in the realm of narrative, the root cause remains hidden behind competing stories.

## Cutting Through with Structure

This is precisely where structured methodologies earn their value:

- **TRIZ** separates the *technical contradiction* from the *human narrative* — what physical parameters are actually in conflict?
- **Six Sigma** demands *data* over *opinion* — measurement replaces testimony
- **Systems thinking** maps the *interactions* between perspectives rather than choosing a winner

The goal isn't to determine whose story is correct. It's to identify the systemic contradiction that all stories point toward — and resolve it.

The Rashomon Effect isn't a failure of communication. It's a signal that the problem is more complex than any single perspective can capture. The right response is not to pick a side — it's to use a method that transcends sides.
